🔬 Triple Science

Biology

Cell Structure and Function

Cells are the basic units of life. Plant cells contain a cell wall, chloroplasts, and a large vacuole. Animal cells contain a nucleus, mitochondria, and other organelles. The nucleus contains DNA and controls cell functions.

Photosynthesis

Process where plants use light energy to convert CO₂ and water into glucose and oxygen. Occurs in chloroplasts. Formula: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Respiration

Process cells use to release energy from glucose. Aerobic respiration produces more ATP than anaerobic respiration. Glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + energy

Genetics & DNA

DNA carries genetic information. Genes are segments of DNA that code for proteins. Chromosomes contain coiled DNA. During reproduction, genetic material is passed to offspring.

Evolution

Organisms change over time through natural selection. Organisms with advantageous traits survive and reproduce. Over many generations, this leads to adaptation and speciation.

Chemistry

Atomic Structure

Atoms contain a nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons. Protons have positive charge, electrons negative, neutrons neutral. Atomic number = number of protons. Mass number = protons + neutrons.

Chemical Bonding

Ionic bonds form between metals and non-metals through electron transfer. Covalent bonds form between non-metals through electron sharing. Metallic bonds occur between metal atoms.

Reactions & Energy

Exothermic reactions release energy (burning, neutralization). Endothermic reactions absorb energy (melting, evaporation). Catalysts speed up reactions without being consumed.

Acids & Bases

Acids have pH < 7, turn litmus red, and produce H⁺ ions. Bases have pH > 7, turn litmus blue, and produce OH⁻ ions. Neutralization: acid + base → salt + water

The Periodic Table

Elements arranged by atomic number and properties. Groups (vertical columns) have similar properties. Periods (horizontal rows) show trends in properties.

Physics

Forces & Motion

Newton's First Law: objects at rest stay at rest unless acted upon by force. Second Law: F = ma (force equals mass times acceleration). Third Law: every action has an equal and opposite reaction.

Energy

Kinetic energy: energy of motion (KE = ½mv²). Potential energy: stored energy based on position. Energy is conserved—can be converted between forms but not created or destroyed.

Waves

Waves transfer energy without transferring matter. Transverse waves: oscillation perpendicular to direction of travel (light, water). Longitudinal waves: oscillation parallel to direction (sound, seismic).

Electricity & Magnetism

Circuits contain voltage (potential difference), current (flow of charge), and resistance (opposition to flow). Ohm's Law: V = IR. Magnets have poles; opposite poles attract, same poles repel.

Thermodynamics

Temperature measures average kinetic energy of particles. Heat flows from hot to cold. First Law of Thermodynamics: energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred.

🔢 Mathematics

Algebra & Functions

Equations & Inequalities

Linear equations: ax + b = c. Quadratic equations: ax² + bx + c = 0. Use factoring, completing the square, or quadratic formula to solve.

Indices & Surds

Laws of indices: aⁿ × aᵐ = aⁿ⁺ᵐ, aⁿ ÷ aᵐ = aⁿ⁻ᵐ, (aⁿ)ᵐ = aⁿᵐ. Surds are irrational numbers like √2. Rationalize by multiplying by conjugate.

Sequences & Series

Arithmetic sequences: constant difference between terms. General term: aₙ = a₁ + (n-1)d. Geometric sequences: constant ratio between terms. Sum formulas apply for arithmetic and geometric series.

Geometry & Trigonometry

Angles & Shapes

Angles in a triangle sum to 180°. Angles in a quadrilateral sum to 360°. Area formulas: triangle = ½bh, circle = πr², rectangle = lw

Trigonometry

SOHCAHTOA: sin = opposite/hypotenuse, cos = adjacent/hypotenuse, tan = opposite/adjacent. Use in right-angled triangles to find angles and sides.

Coordinate Geometry

Distance between points: √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²]. Midpoint: ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2). Gradient/slope: (y₂-y₁)/(x₂-x₁)

Calculus & Statistics

Differentiation (Calculus)

Derivative shows rate of change. Power rule: d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹. Used to find gradients, maximum/minimum values, and rate of change.

Statistics & Probability

Mean: average of all values. Median: middle value. Mode: most frequent value. Standard deviation: measure of spread. Probability: number of favorable outcomes / total outcomes.

Vectors

Vectors have magnitude and direction. Addition: add components. Scalar product: a·b = |a||b|cos(θ). Vectors used in physics and engineering.

📖 English Language & Literature

English Language

Grammar & Syntax

Parts of speech: noun (person/place/thing), verb (action), adjective (describes noun), adverb (describes verb), preposition, pronoun, conjunction. Sentences have subject and predicate.

Punctuation & Spelling

Apostrophes for contractions and possession. Commas separate clauses and items in lists. Semicolons connect independent clauses. Colons introduce lists or explanations.

Writing Skills

Formal writing uses third person and technical language. Informal writing is conversational. Academic writing requires evidence and proper citations. Persuasive writing includes rhetorical devices.

Linguistic Analysis

Identify language features: metaphors, similes, alliteration, onomatopoeia. Analyze how writers use language for effect. Consider tone, register, and audience awareness.

English Literature

Literary Devices

Metaphor: direct comparison (life is a journey). Simile: comparison with 'like' or 'as'. Personification: giving human qualities to non-human things. Imagery: descriptive language appealing to senses.

Character Analysis

Analyze motivations, development, and relationships. Static characters don't change; dynamic characters do. Consider how dialogue reveals character. Archetypes are recurring character types.

Theme & Plot

Theme is the central message or idea. Plot structure: exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution. Symbolism: objects represent abstract ideas.

Critical Reading

Close reading examines specific details for meaning. Contextual reading considers historical and cultural background. Thematic reading identifies central ideas and their development.

Writing & Poetry

Poetry Techniques

Rhyme scheme: pattern of end rhymes. Meter: rhythmic pattern of stressed/unstressed syllables. Rhyme types: perfect rhyme (cat/bat), near rhyme (home/come), internal rhyme within a line.

Poetic Forms

Sonnet: 14 lines with specific rhyme scheme. Haiku: 3 lines (5-7-5 syllables). Free verse: no regular rhyme or meter. Ballad: narrative poem set to music.

Essay Writing

Structure: introduction (thesis statement), body paragraphs (evidence + analysis), conclusion (summary). Use topic sentences and transitions. Support claims with textual evidence.

👥 Sociology

Sociological Foundations

What is Sociology?

Study of human society, social relationships, and group behavior. Examines how individuals interact in groups and how society functions. Considers culture, institutions, and social change.

Key Sociologists & Theories

Marx: conflict theory, class struggle. Durkheim: social solidarity, cohesion. Weber: social action, authority. Functionalism: society as interconnected system. Conflict theory: society based on power struggles.

Culture & Society

Culture includes values, beliefs, norms, customs, language, and artifacts. Socialization is process of learning cultural norms. Enculturation: learning one's own culture. Acculturation: adapting to a new culture.

Social Structure & Inequality

Social Stratification

Hierarchical arrangement of groups based on inequality. Social class: economic position and lifestyle. Caste: hereditary social status. Status: social position; roles: expected behaviors. Meritocracy: advancement based on ability.

Social Inequality

Inequality in wealth, income, education, and opportunity. Systemic factors perpetuate inequality. Gender inequality: discrimination based on sex. Racial/ethnic discrimination affects opportunities and outcomes.

Institutions

Family: primary social group. Education: transmits knowledge and values. Economy: produces and distributes goods. Religion: provides meaning and community. Government: maintains order and provides services.

Social Processes & Change

Socialization & Identity

Primary socialization: family in early childhood. Secondary socialization: peers, school, work. Agents of socialization shape values, beliefs, and behaviors. Self: sense of individual identity develops through interaction.

Deviance & Social Control

Deviance: violation of social norms. Crime: violation of laws. Sanctions: rewards for conformity, punishments for deviance. Formal control: laws and institutions. Informal control: family and community approval.

Social Change

Caused by technology, conflict, innovation, and diffusion. Modernization: transition from traditional to modern society. Globalization: worldwide interconnection. Social movements seek to create change.

✨ Religion & Philosophy

Major World Religions

Christianity

Based on teachings of Jesus Christ. Holy book: Bible (Old & New Testament). Main denominations: Catholic, Protestant, Orthodox. Central beliefs: monotheistic, Jesus as Son of God, salvation through faith.

Islam

Founded by Prophet Muhammad. Holy book: Qur'an. Five Pillars: belief, prayer, charity, fasting, pilgrimage. Monotheistic (Allah is God). Practices: daily prayers, Ramadan fasting, Hajj pilgrimage.

Judaism

Founded by Abraham. Holy books: Torah, Talmud, Tanakh. Monotheistic (one God). Practices: Sabbath observance, Passover, Yom Kippur. Emphasis on Torah study and ethical living.

Buddhism

Founded by Siddhartha Gautama (Buddha). Not monotheistic; focuses on enlightenment. Four Noble Truths: suffering, cause, cessation, path. Eightfold Path guides ethical living.

Hinduism

Ancient religion with complex theology. Polytheistic with many deities. Key concepts: Brahman (ultimate reality), karma, dharma, reincarnation. Vedas are sacred texts.

Religious Concepts

Key Beliefs & Practices

Monotheism: belief in one God. Polytheism: belief in multiple gods. Atheism: disbelief in deities. Agnosticism: belief that God's existence is unknowable. Sacred texts guide believers. Rituals mark important occasions.

Ethics & Morality

Religious ethics provide moral frameworks. Ten Commandments in Judeo-Christian tradition. Karma in Eastern religions links actions to consequences. Golden Rule: treat others as you wish to be treated.

Religious Diversity

Interfaith dialogue promotes understanding. Syncretism: blending of religions. Diaspora: dispersal of religious communities. Religious pluralism: coexistence of different faiths. Religious tolerance supports peaceful coexistence.

Philosophy of Religion

Arguments for God's Existence

Cosmological argument: universe requires a cause. Teleological argument: design suggests designer. Ontological argument: concept of God implies existence. Problem of evil: challenges existence of all-powerful, benevolent God.

Philosophical Questions

Does God exist? Nature of God? Free will vs. determinism. Theodicy: justifying God's allowance of suffering. Religious experience and validity of spiritual claims.

Secularism

Separation of religion and state. Secular ethics based on reason, not scripture. Humanism: human-centered worldview. Naturalism: only natural world exists; no supernatural.

🌍 Geography

Physical Geography

Geomorphology (Landforms)

Erosion: wearing away by water, wind, ice. Weathering: breakdown of rock in place. Deposition: material settles in new location. Processes create mountains, valleys, plains, and deltas.

Climate & Weather

Climate: long-term weather patterns. Weather: short-term atmospheric conditions. Temperature, precipitation, wind determine climate. Climate zones: tropical, temperate, polar. Climate change from greenhouse gases.

Water Systems

Water cycle: evaporation, condensation, precipitation, collection. Rivers flow downhill, eroding and depositing. Oceans cover 71% of Earth; currents transport heat. Groundwater in aquifers supplies freshwater.

Ecosystems & Biomes

Ecosystems: communities of organisms and their environment. Biomes: large-scale ecosystems (rainforest, desert, tundra). Food chains and webs transfer energy. Biodiversity supports ecosystem health.

Human Geography

Population Geography

Demographics study population characteristics. Birth rate: births per 1000 people. Death rate: deaths per 1000 people. Population growth varies by development level. Migration: movement between regions.

Economic Geography

Primary sector: agriculture, mining, fishing. Secondary sector: manufacturing. Tertiary sector: services. Quaternary sector: information, research. Globalization connects economies worldwide.

Urban Geography

Urbanization: growth of cities. Urban hierarchy: cities by size and function. CBD (Central Business District) core of cities. Suburbanization: expansion outward. Urban issues: congestion, housing, infrastructure.

Political & Cultural Geography

Geopolitics: how geography influences politics. Boundaries: borders between territories. Sovereignty: control over territory. Nationalism: pride in nation-state. Cultural differences shape societies and conflicts.

Environmental Geography

Environmental Issues

Climate change: global warming from greenhouse gases. Deforestation: forest loss for agriculture/development. Pollution: air, water, soil contamination. Resource depletion: overuse of finite resources.

Sustainability

Sustainable development: meets current needs without harming future generations. Renewable resources: can be replenished (solar, wind, water). Conservation: protecting biodiversity and ecosystems.

Human-Environment Interaction

Humans modify environment: agriculture, construction, industry. Natural hazards: earthquakes, hurricanes, floods. Environmental management: reducing human impact. Balance needed between development and conservation.
